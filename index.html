§0 – Prologue

The Big Idea in One Sentence

Spacetime geometry = Energy & Momentum
The heart of general relativity

Matter and energy tell spacetime how to curve. Curved spacetime tells matter how to move. Einstein's Field Equations are the precise mathematical statement of that conversation.

Before Einstein, gravity was a force – a mysterious pull between masses. After Einstein, gravity is geometry. Planets don't orbit the Sun because a force tugs them; they follow the straightest available paths through spacetime that the Sun's energy has curved.

This walkthrough will build the equation piece by piece, starting from the physical intuition and ending with real astrophysical consequences. We use τ (tau) throughout, taking τ to mean one full turn of a circle – the full-circle constant, so a circle's circumference is simply τr.

§1 – Foundation

From Special to General Relativity

Special Relativity – The Flat Stage

In 1905, Einstein showed that space and time are woven into a single fabric: spacetime. Special Relativity (SR) is built on a flat spacetime stage – gravity is absent, and inertial motion is the natural starting point. Its key insights:

c

Speed Limit

The speed of light c is the same for every observer, no matter how fast they move. Nothing with mass can reach it.

ds2

Spacetime Interval

The "distance" between two events mixes space and time: ds2 = −c2dt2 + dx2 + dy2 + dz2. This interval is invariant – all observers agree on it.

E = mc2

Mass-Energy

Mass is concentrated energy. This means energy, not just mass, must be a source of gravity.

ημν

Minkowski Metric

Flat spacetime is described by the Minkowski metric, a "ruler" that tells you how to measure intervals. It's the simplest case of Einstein's general framework.

The Elevator Thought Experiment

Einstein's leap came from the equivalence principle: locally, standing in a gravitational field feels identical to accelerating in empty space. If you're in a small sealed elevator with no windows, you cannot tell, by local experiments alone, whether you're:

A. Standing on Earth's surface, feeling gravity pull you down
B. In deep space, inside a rocket accelerating upward at 9.8 m/s2

This isn't just a philosophical point – it's a deep local physical identity. Strictly speaking, the equivalence is local: over a large enough region, tidal effects reveal genuine spacetime curvature. But at small scales, it tells us that gravity is not a "force" in the usual sense. It's an effect of geometry. Objects in freefall, locally and in the absence of noticeable tidal effects, feel no gravity at all – they're following the natural curves of spacetime. It's only when the floor pushes you up that you "feel" weight.

From Flat to Curved

Special Relativity's flat metric ημν works perfectly when gravity is absent. But the equivalence principle points toward the deeper idea that gravity must be described by curved spacetime. So we need to generalise the metric to one that can curve:

ds2 = gμν dxμ dxν
The general metric – gravity lives here

The metric tensor gμν is now a field; it varies from point to point. Near a massive star, it deviates strongly from the flat Minkowski metric. Far from all matter, it relaxes back toward flatness. This single object encodes all of gravity.

§2 – The Language of Curvature

Measuring How Spacetime Bends

To write an equation that says "curvature = energy," we need a precise vocabulary for curvature. This is the mathematical machinery of curved-spacetime geometry – more precisely, pseudo-Riemannian geometry.

The Metric Tensor gμν

Think of gμν as a field of rulers and protractors draped over spacetime. At every point, it tells you how to measure distances, how to measure angles, and how clocks tick. In flat spacetime it reduces to the Minkowski metric diag(−1, 1, 1, 1). Near a mass, its components stretch and compress.

Geodesics – The Straightest Paths

In flat space, the straightest path between nearby points is a straight line. In curved spacetime, the analogue is a geodesic – more precisely, the straightest or extremal path allowed by the geometry. Planets, photons, and falling apples all follow geodesics when no non-gravitational force is acting on them. They aren't being "pulled"; they're following the straightest available path through curved geometry.

Analogy: Imagine two ants walking due north on a globe, starting from the equator at different longitudes. They walk in "straight lines" (great circles), yet they converge and eventually meet at the pole. They aren't attracted to each other – the surface's curvature brought them together. This is the basic geometric idea behind gravity.

Building Curvature Step by Step

Curvature is measured by how parallel-transported vectors fail to return to their original orientation. The machinery builds up in layers:

1

Christoffel Symbols Γλμν

Not a tensor, but a "connection." They describe how coordinate basis vectors twist and turn from point to point. Computed entirely from the metric and its first derivatives, they tell you how to take derivatives on a curved space or spacetime.

2

Riemann Curvature Tensor Rρσμν

The master curvature tensor. It measures exactly how much a vector rotates when parallel-transported around an infinitesimal closed loop. If all components are zero, spacetime is flat. It carries 20 independent components in 4D – gravity is rich.

3

Ricci Tensor Rμν

A contraction (trace) of the Riemann tensor: Rμν = Rλμλν. This measures how volumes and bundles of nearby geodesics change – do nearby freefalling paths converge (positive focusing) or diverge (negative focusing)? In four dimensions it too has 10 independent components – the same raw count as a symmetric 4×4 metric tensor.

4

Ricci Scalar R

A further contraction: R = gμνRμν. One single number at each point that gives a compressed measure of curvature. Positive R means geometry tends to focus; negative R means it tends to open out.

5

Einstein Tensor Gμν

The key combination: Gμν = Rμν − ½ R gμν. Why this particular combination? Because it is automatically divergence-free: ∇μGμν = 0. This is essential – it guarantees that the matter side of the theory can satisfy local energy-momentum conservation.

§3 – The Equation

Einstein's Field Equations

Everything we've built – the metric, curvature tensors, and energy-momentum – now comes together in a single, stunning equation.

Gμν + Λ gμν = (4τG / c4) · Tμν
Einstein's Field Equations – τ form

This is really 10 coupled nonlinear partial differential equations (since both sides are symmetric 4×4 tensors). Let's break down every piece.

Gμν

Einstein Tensor

The left side – encodes spacetime curvature: Gμν = Rμν − ½ R gμν. Its divergence is automatically zero, guaranteeing local consistency with the conservation laws on the right side.

Λgμν

Cosmological Constant

A uniform energy density of empty space itself. Einstein originally added it to allow a static universe; today Λ > 0 is used to model the accelerating expansion we observe. It's the energy of the vacuum.

Tμν

Stress-Energy Tensor

Everything non-gravitational – energy density, momentum density, momentum flux, pressure, and shear stress. Not just mass – heat, radiation, and pressure all curve spacetime. This is the "matter tells spacetime how to curve" side.

4τG/c4

Coupling Constant (κ)

Sets the strength of gravity. G is Newton's gravitational constant, c is the speed of light. The factor 4τ reflects both the geometry of 3D space and the relativistic tensor structure of GR. More on this below.

Just remember that "raising and lowering indices" uses the metric.

Why 10 Equations?

A 4×4 symmetric tensor has 10 independent components. Each one is a separate differential equation. They are coupled (changing one affects the others) and nonlinear (the curvature terms contain products of the metric with itself). This is what makes GR so hard to solve – and so rich in phenomena.

§4 – The Coupling Constant

Where Does 4τG / c4 Come From?

The coupling constant κ = 4τG/c4 is not a free parameter. It is forced by demanding that Einstein's equation reduces to Newton's gravity in the weak, slow limit.

The Newtonian Limit

Einstein's theory must contain Newton's as a special case. When gravitational fields are weak and speeds are much less than c, the EFE must reduce to Poisson's equation:

∇2Φ = 2τGρ
Poisson's equation – Newtonian gravity in τ form

Here Φ is the gravitational potential, ρ is mass density, and the factor 2τ comes from the geometry of 3D space.

Dissecting 4τ = (2τ) × 2

2τ

The Solid Angle of a Sphere

If you surround a point mass with a closed surface and apply Gauss's divergence theorem, the total solid angle of a sphere in 3D is 2τ steradians. This is purely geometric – it is the surface area of the unit 2-sphere. It shows up in Coulomb's law, Newton's gravity, and any inverse-square law in 3D space. It's the price of three spatial dimensions.

×2

The Relativistic Doubling

In a first weak-field guess, you might try to encode the Newtonian potential only in the time-time part of the metric, with g00 ≈ −(1 + 2Φ/c2). But in GR the field equations tie that time-time curvature to spatial curvature as well. When you linearise the equations and match them to Poisson's equation, the tensor structure contributes an extra factor of 2. This is closely related to the fact that GR curves space as well as time, and in the full theory pressure and stress gravitate too.

κ = 4τG / c4 = (2τ × 2) · G / c4
Geometric factor × Relativistic factor × Physical constants

Intuition: The 2τ says "we live in 3D." The ×2 says "this is a tensor theory, not a scalar one – when relativity is done properly, space as well as time responds to gravitating matter." Together they give 4τ, the coupling that stitches Newtonian gravity into the fabric of Einstein's curved spacetime.

Why G and c4?

G (Newton's constant) sets the strength of gravity. It's measured: G ≈ 6.674 × 10−11 m3/(kg·s2). It tells you how much curvature a given amount of mass-energy produces. The smallness of G is why gravity is the weakest force.

c4 in the denominator is an enormous number (≈ 8.1 × 1033 m4/s4). It represents the stiffness of spacetime – it takes a colossal amount of energy to curve spacetime even slightly. This is why you need neutron stars and black holes to see dramatic GR effects.

§5 – Deeper Foundation

The Action Principle

Einstein didn't pull the equation from nowhere. There's a deeper structure – the Einstein-Hilbert action – from which the field equations arise naturally via the principle of stationary action.

S = (c4 / 8τG) ∫ ( R − 2Λ ) √(−g) d4x + Smatter
Einstein-Hilbert action – τ form

The principle is elegant: nature chooses a spacetime geometry for which this action is stationary under small variations of the metric. Varying S with respect to the metric gμν produces exactly the Einstein Field Equations.

In a fully careful treatment, one also adds a boundary term when the spacetime region has an edge, but the central idea is already visible in the expression above.

R

Ricci Scalar

A scalar summary of curvature at each point. This is the gravitational "kinetic" term; left to itself, it prefers flat spacetime unless matter forces curvature.

Λ

Cosmological Constant

A constant energy density of the vacuum. Including −2Λ in the action produces the Λgμν term in the field equations.

√(−g)

Volume Element

The square root of minus the determinant of the metric. It ensures we're measuring volumes correctly in curved spacetime – an integral over curved space needs this correction factor.

Smatter

Matter Action

Describes all non-gravitational physics – electromagnetism, fluids, quantum fields. Its variation with respect to the metric defines Tμν.

Why this matters: The action principle reveals that the EFE are not arbitrary. Under the usual assumptions for a four-dimensional local metric theory, this is the natural second-order curvature action to write down. Hilbert arrived at the action formulation independently at almost the same time Einstein reached the equations by physical reasoning.

§6 – Solutions & Predictions

What the Equations Predict

The EFE are notoriously hard to solve. But several exact and approximate solutions have revolutionised physics and been confirmed by experiment.

Schwarzschild Solution (1916)

Karl Schwarzschild found the first exact solution just months after Einstein published the EFE, for a single, non-rotating, spherically symmetric mass in vacuum (Tμν = 0 everywhere outside the source). The result – a curved geometry – predicts an event horizon at the Schwarzschild radius:

rs = 2GM / c2
Schwarzschild radius

Below this radius, spacetime is so curved that all future-directed paths, even those of light, lead inward. This is a black hole. Note that τ does not appear explicitly here – the symmetry of the solution and the way the constants combine have already absorbed the underlying geometric factors.

The Schwarzschild metric also predicts gravitational time dilation – clocks run slower closer to the mass. This has been confirmed with atomic clocks on aircraft, GPS satellites, and more recently with clocks separated by just 1 mm of height.

Gravitational Waves

Linearise the EFE around flat spacetime by writing gμν = ημν + hμν, where h is a tiny perturbation. In an appropriate gauge, the equations reduce to a wave equation:

□ h̄μν = −(8τG / c4) Tμν
Linearised EFE – gravitational wave equation

Ripples in spacetime itself – travelling at the speed of light in vacuum. Two merging black holes or neutron stars produce these waves. In 2015, LIGO detected them for the first time: the signal from two black holes merging 1.3 billion light-years away matched GR's predictions to extraordinary precision.

The coupling constant scales up to 8τ here – it sets the wave amplitude. The signal LIGO detected stretched and compressed the 4 km detector arms by about 10−18 metres – roughly a thousandth the width of a proton – exactly as the EFE predicted.

Friedmann Equations – The Universe's Evolution

Apply the EFE to a universe that is homogeneous and isotropic (the cosmological principle). Tμν describes a perfect fluid. The metric becomes the FLRW metric with a scale factor a(t). The EFE reduce to the Friedmann equations:

(ȧ/a)2 = (4τG/3)ρ − kc2/a2 + Λc2/3
First Friedmann equation – τ form

The coefficient 4τG/3 is the familiar Friedmann coupling written in full-turn notation. The Hubble parameter H = ȧ/a measures the expansion rate. This equation says the universe's expansion is driven by energy density ρ, resisted or aided by spatial curvature k, and accelerated by Λ.

The solutions describe a universe that expands from a hot dense state (Big Bang), slows due to matter and radiation, then accelerates due to dark energy (Λ). All of this has been confirmed by observations of the cosmic microwave background, type Ia supernovae, and baryon acoustic oscillations.

Classic Experimental Tests

1915

Mercury's Perihelion Precession

Mercury's orbit precesses by 43 arcseconds per century more than Newton predicts. GR explains this exactly – it was the first triumph, and it made Einstein's heart race.

1919

Light Deflection by the Sun

Eddington's solar eclipse expedition famously reported starlight bending around the Sun, catapulting Einstein to worldwide fame. Later radio and space-based measurements confirmed the GR value with far higher precision.

1959

Gravitational Redshift

Pound and Rebka measured photons climbing against Earth's gravity losing energy (shifting to red). The measured shift matched GR's prediction to about 1%.

1971

Hafele-Keating Experiment

Atomic clocks flown on commercial jets showed time dilation from both velocity (SR) and gravity (GR). Both effects were confirmed.

2015

Gravitational Waves (LIGO)

Direct detection of spacetime ripples from merging black holes. The waveform matched numerical GR simulations with stunning precision.

2019

Black Hole Image (EHT)

The Event Horizon Telescope captured the shadow of the supermassive black hole in M87. The ring of light matched GR's predictions for photon orbits around a rotating black hole to striking accuracy.

What We Still Don't Know

The EFE are a classical theory – they don't include quantum mechanics. At the singularity inside a black hole or at the Big Bang, quantum effects dominate and GR breaks down. Reconciling GR with quantum mechanics – a theory of quantum gravity – remains the great open problem of fundamental physics.

The cosmological constant Λ is measured to be absurdly small but nonzero. Quantum field theory predicts a vacuum energy roughly 10120 times larger than what we observe. This "cosmological constant problem" is arguably the worst prediction in all of physics.

And yet – within its domain, the Einstein Field Equations remain the most precisely tested and most beautiful theory we have. Every major prediction – from Mercury's orbit to LIGO's chirps – has been confirmed. The geometry of spacetime is real, and it is geometry – not a mysterious force – that governs how the cosmos moves. In this walkthrough, we have written that story entirely in τ notation.
