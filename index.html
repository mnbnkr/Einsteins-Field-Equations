§0 – Prologue

The Big Idea in One Sentence

Spacetime geometry = Energy & Momentum
The heart of general relativity

Matter and energy tell spacetime how to curve. Curved spacetime tells matter how to move. Einstein's Field Equations are the precise mathematical statement of that conversation.

Before Einstein, gravity was a force, a mysterious pull between masses. After Einstein, gravity is geometry. Planets don't orbit the Sun because a force tugs them; they follow the straightest possible paths through spacetime that the Sun's energy has curved.

This walkthrough will build the equation piece by piece, starting from the physical intuition and ending with real astrophysical consequences. We use τ (tau) = 2π throughout, one full turn of a circle, arguably the more natural constant.

§1 – Foundation

From Special to General Relativity

Special Relativity: The Flat Stage

In 1905, Einstein showed that space and time are woven into a single fabric: spacetime. Special Relativity (SR) works on a flat stage, no gravity, no acceleration. Its key insights:

c

Speed Limit

The speed of light c is identical for every observer, no matter how fast they move. Nothing with mass can reach it.

ds²

Spacetime Interval

The "distance" between two events mixes space and time: ds² = −c²dt² + dx² + dy² + dz². This interval is invariant; all observers agree on it.

E = mc²

Mass-Energy

Mass is concentrated energy. This means energy, not just mass, must be a source of gravity.

ημν

Minkowski Metric

Flat spacetime is described by the Minkowski metric, a "ruler" that tells you how to measure intervals. It's the trivial case of Einstein's general framework.

The Elevator Thought Experiment

Einstein's leap came from the equivalence principle: standing in a gravitational field feels identical to accelerating in empty space. If you're in an elevator with no windows, you cannot tell whether you're:

A. Standing on Earth's surface, feeling gravity pull you down
B. In deep space, inside a rocket accelerating upward at 9.8 m/s²

This isn't just a philosophical point; it's a deep physical identity. It means gravity is not a "force" in the usual sense. It's an effect of geometry. Objects in freefall feel no gravity at all; they're following the natural curves of spacetime. It's only when the floor pushes you up that you "feel" weight.

From Flat to Curved

Special Relativity's flat metric ημν works perfectly when there's no gravity. But the equivalence principle tells us that gravity = curved spacetime. So we need to generalise the metric to one that can curve:

ds² = gμν dxμ dxν
The general metric – gravity lives here

The metric tensor gμν is now a field; it varies from point to point. Near a massive star, it deviates strongly from the flat Minkowski metric. Far from all matter, it relaxes back to flat. This single object encodes all of gravity.

§2 – The Language of Curvature

Measuring How Spacetime Bends

To write an equation that says "curvature = energy," we need a precise vocabulary for curvature. This is the mathematical machinery of Riemannian geometry.

The Metric Tensor gμν

Think of gμν as a field of rulers and protractors draped over spacetime. At every point, it tells you: how to measure distances, how to measure angles, and how clocks tick. In flat spacetime it reduces to the Minkowski metric diag(−1, 1, 1, 1). Near a mass, its components stretch and compress.

Geodesics – The Straightest Paths

In flat space, the shortest path between two points is a straight line. In curved spacetime, it's a geodesic. Planets, photons, and falling apples all follow geodesics. They aren't being "pulled"; they're following the straightest available path through curved geometry.

Analogy: Imagine two ants walking due north on a globe, starting from the equator at different longitudes. They walk in "straight lines" (great circles), yet they converge and eventually meet at the pole. They aren't attracted to each other; the surface's curvature brought them together. This is gravity.

Building Curvature Step by Step

Curvature is measured by how parallel-transported vectors fail to return to their original orientation. The machinery builds up in layers:

1

Christoffel Symbols Γλμν

Not a tensor, but a "connection." They describe how coordinate basis vectors twist and turn from point to point. Computed entirely from the metric and its first derivatives, they tell you how to take derivatives on a curved surface.

2

Riemann Curvature Tensor Rρσμν

The master curvature tensor. It measures exactly how much a vector rotates when parallel-transported around an infinitesimal closed loop. If all components are zero, spacetime is flat. It carries 20 independent components in 4D; gravity is rich.

3

Ricci Tensor Rμν

A contraction (trace) of the Riemann tensor: Rμν = Rλμλν. This measures how volumes change: do nearby geodesics converge (positive curvature) or diverge (negative curvature)? It captures 10 degrees of freedom, matching the metric's 10 independent components.

4

Ricci Scalar R

A further contraction: R = gμνRμν. One single number at each point that gives the overall "total curvature." Positive R means space tends to close in; negative R means it spreads out.

5

Einstein Tensor Gμν

The key combination: Gμν = Rμν − ½Rgμν. Why this particular combination? Because it is automatically divergence-free: ∇μGμν = 0. This is essential; it guarantees that energy-momentum is conserved.

§3 – The Equation

Einstein's Field Equations

Everything we've built, the metric, curvature tensors, and energy-momentum, now comes together in a single, stunning equation.

Gμν + Λ gμν = 4τG / c4 · Tμν
Einstein's Field Equations – τ form (τ = 2π)

This is really 10 coupled nonlinear partial differential equations (since both sides are symmetric 4×4 tensors). Let's break down every piece.

Gμν

Einstein Tensor

The left side. Encodes spacetime curvature: Gμν = Rμν − ½Rgμν. Its divergence is automatically zero, guaranteeing energy conservation on the right side.

Λgμν

Cosmological Constant

A uniform energy density of empty space itself. Einstein originally added it to allow a static universe; today Λ > 0 drives the accelerating expansion we observe. It's the energy of the vacuum.

Tμν

Stress-Energy Tensor

Everything non-gravitational: energy density, momentum flux, pressure, and shear stress. Not just mass; heat, radiation, and pressure all curve spacetime. This is the "matter tells space how to curve" side.

4τG/c⁴

Coupling Constant (κ)

Sets the strength of gravity. G is Newton's gravitational constant, c is the speed of light. The τ and the factor of 4 arise from the geometry of 3D space and the tensor nature of GR. More on this below.

Why 10 Equations?

A 4×4 symmetric tensor has 10 independent components. Each one is a separate differential equation. They are coupled (changing one affects the others) and nonlinear (the curvature terms contain products of the metric with itself). This is what makes GR so hard to solve and so rich in phenomena.

§4 – The Coupling Constant

Where Does 4τG / c⁴ Come From?

The coupling constant κ = 4τG/c⁴ is not a free parameter. It is forced by demanding that Einstein's equation reduces to Newton's gravity in the weak, slow limit.

The Newtonian Limit

Einstein's theory must contain Newton's as a special case. When gravitational fields are weak and speeds are much less than c, the EFE must reduce to Poisson's equation:

∇²Φ = 2τGρ
Poisson's equation – the Newtonian target (τ form)

Here Φ is the gravitational potential, ρ is mass density, and the factor 2τ (= 4π in traditional notation) comes from the geometry of 3D space.

Dissecting 4τ = (2τ) × 2

2τ

The Solid Angle of a Sphere

If you surround a point mass with a closed surface and apply Gauss's divergence theorem, the total solid angle of a sphere in 3D is 2τ steradians (= 4π). This is purely geometric: it's the surface area of a unit sphere. It shows up in Coulomb's law, Newton's gravity, and any inverse-square law in 3D space. It's the price of three spatial dimensions.

×2

The Relativistic Doubling

In Newtonian gravity, only time is warped (clocks slow in a gravitational well). In GR, both space and time are curved. When you linearise the metric g₀₀ ≈ −(1 + 2Φ/c²) and compute the Einstein tensor, the tensor structure introduces an extra factor of 2. This factor reflects that pressure gravitates too: the full energy-momentum tensor contributes, not just mass.

κ = 4τG / c⁴ = (2τ × 2) · G / c⁴
Geometric factor × Relativistic factor × Physical constants

Intuition: The 2τ says "we live in 3D." The ×2 says "this is a tensor theory, not a scalar one; space curves, not just time." Together they give 4τ, the coupling that stitches Newtonian gravity into the fabric of Einstein's curved spacetime.

Why G and c⁴?

G (Newton's constant) sets the strength of gravity. It's measured: G ≈ 6.674 × 10⁻¹¹ m³/(kg·s²). It tells you how much curvature a given amount of mass-energy produces. The smallness of G is why gravity is the weakest force.

c⁴ in the denominator is an enormous number (≈ 8.1 × 10³³ m⁴/s⁴). It represents the stiffness of spacetime: it takes a colossal amount of energy to curve spacetime even slightly. This is why you need neutron stars and black holes to see dramatic GR effects.

§5 – Deeper Foundation

The Action Principle

Einstein didn't pull the equation from nowhere. There's a deeper structure, the Einstein-Hilbert action, from which the field equations arise naturally via the principle of least action.

S = ∫ ( R − 2Λ ) / (4τG/c⁴) · √(−g) · d⁴x + Smatter
Einstein-Hilbert action – τ form

The principle is elegant: nature chooses the spacetime geometry that extremises (makes stationary) this action. Varying S with respect to the metric gμν produces exactly the Einstein Field Equations.

R

Ricci Scalar

Total curvature at each point. This is the gravitational "kinetic energy" term; it prefers flat spacetime unless matter forces curvature.

Λ

Cosmological Constant

A constant energy density of the vacuum. Including −2Λ in the action produces the Λgμν term in the field equations.

√(−g)

Volume Element

The square root of minus the determinant of the metric. It ensures we're measuring volumes correctly in curved spacetime: an integral over curved space needs this correction factor.

Smatter

Matter Action

Describes all non-gravitational physics: electromagnetism, fluids, quantum fields. Its variation with respect to the metric defines Tμν.

Why this matters: The action principle reveals that the EFE are not arbitrary; they are the unique second-order equations for a metric theory of gravity that conserve energy-momentum. Hilbert derived them from the action independently, just five days before Einstein found them by physical reasoning.

§6 – Solutions & Predictions

What the Equations Predict

The EFE are notoriously hard to solve. But several exact and approximate solutions have revolutionised physics and been confirmed by experiment.

Schwarzschild Solution (1916)

Karl Schwarzschild found the first exact solution just months after Einstein published the EFE, for a single, non-rotating, spherically symmetric mass in vacuum (Tμν = 0 everywhere outside the mass). The result: a curved geometry that predicts an event horizon at the Schwarzschild radius:

rs = 2GM / c²
Schwarzschild radius

Below this radius, spacetime is so curved that all paths, even light, lead inward. This is a black hole. Note that τ doesn't appear here; this formula is pure Newton + relativity, with no solid-angle geometry involved.

The Schwarzschild metric also predicts gravitational time dilation: clocks run slower closer to the mass. This has been confirmed with atomic clocks on aircraft, GPS satellites, and more recently with clocks separated by just 1 mm of height.

Gravitational Waves

Linearise the EFE around flat spacetime: gμν = ημν + hμν where h is a tiny perturbation. The equations reduce to a wave equation:

□ h̄μν = −(4τG / c⁴) Tμν
Linearised EFE – gravitational wave equation

Ripples in spacetime itself, travelling at the speed of light. Two merging black holes or neutron stars produce these waves. In 2015, LIGO detected them for the first time: the signal from two black holes merging 1.3 billion light-years away matched GR's predictions to extraordinary precision.

The 4τ coupling appears here too: it sets the wave amplitude. The signal LIGO detected stretched and compressed the 4 km detector arms by about 10⁻¹⁸ metres, a thousandth the width of a proton, exactly as the EFE predicted.

Friedmann Equations – The Universe's Evolution

Apply the EFE to a universe that is homogeneous and isotropic (the cosmological principle). Tμν describes a perfect fluid. The metric becomes the FLRW metric with a scale factor a(t). The EFE reduce to the Friedmann equations:

(ȧ/a)² = (τG/3)ρ − kc²/a² + Λc²/3
First Friedmann equation – τ form

Here τG/3 = 8πG/6 (since τ = 2π). The Hubble parameter H = ȧ/a measures expansion rate. This equation says the universe's expansion is driven by energy density ρ, resisted by spatial curvature k, and accelerated by Λ.

The solutions describe a universe that expands from a hot dense state (Big Bang), slows due to matter and radiation, then accelerates due to dark energy (Λ). All of this has been confirmed by observations of the cosmic microwave background, type Ia supernovae, and baryon acoustic oscillations.

Classic Experimental Tests

1915

Mercury's Perihelion Precession

Mercury's orbit precesses by 43 arcseconds per century more than Newton predicts. GR explains this exactly; it was the first triumph, and it made Einstein's heart race.

1919

Light Deflection by the Sun

Eddington's solar eclipse expedition measured starlight bending around the Sun. GR predicted double Newton's value (because space curves too, not just time; that factor of 2 again). The result matched GR.

1959

Gravitational Redshift

Pound and Rebka measured photons climbing against Earth's gravity losing energy (shifting to red). The measured shift matched GR's prediction to 1%.

1971

Hafele-Keating Experiment

Atomic clocks flown on commercial jets showed time dilation from both velocity (SR) and gravity (GR). Both effects confirmed.

2015

Gravitational Waves (LIGO)

Direct detection of spacetime ripples from merging black holes. The waveform matched numerical GR simulations with stunning precision.

2019

Black Hole Image (EHT)

The Event Horizon Telescope captured the shadow of the supermassive black hole in M87. The ring of light matched GR's predictions for photon orbits around a Kerr black hole.

What We Still Don't Know

The EFE are a classical theory; they don't include quantum mechanics. At the singularity inside a black hole or at the Big Bang, quantum effects dominate and GR breaks down. Reconciling GR with quantum mechanics, a theory of quantum gravity, remains the great open problem of fundamental physics.

The cosmological constant Λ is measured to be absurdly small but nonzero. Quantum field theory predicts a vacuum energy roughly 10¹²⁰ times larger than what we observe. This "cosmological constant problem" is arguably the worst prediction in all of physics.

And yet: within its domain, the Einstein Field Equations remain the most precisely tested and most beautiful theory we have. Every prediction, from Mercury's orbit to LIGO's chirps, has been confirmed. The geometry of spacetime is real, and τ, not a mysterious force, governs how the cosmos moves.
